History

Origins of the Myth of Hou Yi

Hou Yi, also simply known as Yi, known as the greatest archer in Chinese mythology, has origins deeply rooted in both muth and historical allegory. Often depicted as an immortal being who chose to become human, Hou Yi’s most famous tale is the story of the ten suns. According to the myth, during the reign of the mythical Emperor Yao, ten suns existed, taking turns to light the Earth. However, one day, all ten suns appeared together, scorching the Earth and causing widespread devastation. Hou Yi, with his bow made of tiger bones and arrows crafted from dragon tendons, was tasked by the Jade Emperor to restore order. After reasoning with the suns failed, Hou Yi shot down nine of them, sparing only one to ensure the world would still have light. This myth reflects the ancient Chinese values of duty, sacrifice, and the moral responsibilities of leadership.

Historical References

Hou Yi’s legend is not confined to myth; it also intersects with historical references that add depth to his character. In classical Chinese texts, Hou Yi is sometimes portrayed as a tribal leader who played a significant role during the early Xia dynasty. The "Bamboo Annals" describe Hou Yi as a figure who seized power during the reign of King Taikang, capturing the Xia capital while the king was away. This historical depiction aligns with the mythological narratives, suggesting that Hou Yi’s story may have been used to convey lessons about leadership, power, and the challenges faced by rulers. Some scholars even propose that the story of Hou Yi shooting the ten suns might be a symbolic retelling of a historical conflict, reflecting the complex relationship between myth and history in ancient Chinese culture.

Legacy and Influence

Despite his early heroics, the latter part of Hou Yi’s story is marked by tragedy and moral complexity. After his triumphs, Hou Yi was gifted an elixir of immortality by Xiwangmu, the Queen Mother of the West. However, this gift became a source of conflict between Hou Yi and his wife, Chang’e. In one version of the myth, Chang’e, fearing the possibility of Hou Yi becoming a tyrant if he were to live forever, stole and consumed the elixir herself, fleeing to the moon to escape his wrath. In other versions, Hou Yi’s anger and eventual forgiveness of Chang’e are highlighted, with him leaving offerings for her, which evolved into the traditions of the Mid-Autumn Festival.
